The Macro Pulse

India has aggressively advanced the structural build-out of its national Carbon Credit Trading Scheme (CCTS), establishing a legal and operational framework for an Indian Carbon Market (ICM) based on emission-intensity targets. The Energy Conservation (Amendment) Act, 2022, provided the statutory basis for CCTS, shifting from the energy-saving certificates (PAT) to emission-based Carbon Credit Certificates (CCCs) [1][3]. The CCTS, 2023, formally outlined the institutional framework, including Greenhouse Gas Emission Intensity (GEI) targets for seven energy-intensive sectors [4]. Significantly, the Greenhouse Gases Emission Intensity Target Amendment Rules, 2025, notified in January 2026, expanded coverage to additional sectors like secondary aluminium, petroleum refineries, petrochemicals, and textiles, detailing plant-level baselines for 2023-24 and specific targets for 2025-26 and 2026-27 [2]. This expansion broadens the pool of obligated entities to approximately 800 industrial plants implementing 1-3% reduction plans in the first compliance year (Apr 2025 – Mar 2026) [1][2][6]. Further cementing the operational structure, the Central Electricity Regulatory Commission (CERC) notified the Terms and Conditions for Purchase and Sale of Carbon Credit Certificates Regulations, 2026, designating the Grid Controller of India (Grid-India) as the official Registry and the Bureau of Energy Efficiency (BEE) as the Administrator [5][7]. CCCs will be traded on power exchanges, with the Power Minister indicating the first official trades by October 2026 [1][3][5][8]. This regulatory and institutional clarity marks a pivot from planning to operationalization, aligning India's domestic producers with stringent environmental policies in export markets and supporting the nation's NDC target of a 45% reduction in emissions intensity by 2030 versus 2005 [1][3].

02

Friction Points

Nascent Verification Capacity Bottleneck

The CCTS depends on a new ecosystem of Accredited Carbon Verification Agencies (ACVAs) to audit emissions data before CCC issuance [1][4]. The government only 'put in place' accreditation procedures recently, implying a limited pool of qualified agencies [4]. This is a structural constraint as hundreds of diverse industrial plants (~800) will seek verification for their first compliance year (Apr 2025–Mar 2026) within a narrow window (Apr–Jun 2026) [1]. The direct impact will be delays in the verification process for obligated entities, potentially deferring the issuance of Carbon Credit Certificates (CCCs) beyond the targeted October 2026 trading launch [1][8]. Second-order effects include increased verification costs due to high demand and limited supply of ACVAs, impacting initial compliance costs for industries in sectors like cement, aluminium, and textiles [1][2]. Third-order effects could expose systemic data quality issues within industrial plants and lead to compliance breaches if CCCs cannot be verified and issued in time, potentially forcing companies into penalty payments.

[1][4]

Registry and Trading Infrastructure Lag

Grid Controller of India (Grid-India) is designated as the official Registry, but CERC regulations specify that it 'is required to develop and establish the necessary framework' to operate this role [5]. Similarly, CCC transactions must run through power exchanges (e.g., IEX/PXIL), which necessitates adaptation of their listing, clearing, and settlement workflows [5][7]. This indicates that the core digital infrastructure for the market is not yet fully built out or tested, creating a structural dependency on new system development. First-order effects will be potential delays or operational glitches during the initial market launch if Grid-India's registry and the power exchanges' systems are not fully operational and seamlessly integrated by October 2026 [1][5][8]. Second-order effects include a risk of early market illiquidity or inefficient price discovery due to technical friction in trading and settlement [5]. Third-order effects could expose vulnerabilities in data governance and cybersecurity if rapid deployment compromises robustness, impacting investor confidence and the overall integrity of the CCTS.

[1][5][7][8]

Change Management & MRV System Overhaul for Obligated Entities

The CCTS replaces the older Perform, Achieve & Trade (PAT) scheme, fundamentally shifting the compliance metric from energy savings (kWh) to Greenhouse Gas Emission Intensity (tCO₂/unit output) [1]. This requires approximately 800 obligated industrial plants to overhaul their internal data systems, monitoring protocols, and reporting processes [1]. The January 2026 expansion to complex sectors like refineries, petrochemicals, and textiles, each with distinct production metrics and detailed plant-level baselines, further amplifies this internal data management overhead [2]. The immediate impact is significant internal change management friction and capital expenditure for obligated entities to implement new MRV systems and training [1][2]. Second-order effects will be a higher probability of inaccurate or delayed emissions reporting by individual plants, creating downstream challenges for ACVAs during verification and potentially delaying CCC issuance [1][4]. Third-order effects could lead to non-compliance for entities unable to adapt quickly, or inconsistent data quality across the market, undermining the credibility of the CCCs and their price formation in the initial years.

What Changed This Quarter

Since Q4 2025, the Indian Carbon Market has undergone two significant structural shifts moving it from a conceptual framework to an operational reality. First, the Central Electricity Regulatory Commission (CERC) notified the Terms and Conditions for Purchase and Sale of Carbon Credit Certificates Regulations, 2026, formally defining the trading framework, designating Grid Controller of India as the official Registry, and the Bureau of Energy Efficiency (BEE) as the Administrator [5][7]. This critical regulatory step provides the definitive plumbing for the market, enabling power exchanges to adapt their systems and setting a clear path for compliance CCC trading by October 2026 [1][5][8]. Second, the Greenhouse Gases Emission Intensity Target Amendment Rules, 2025, were notified in January 2026, materially expanding the scope of the CCTS by adding four complex sectors (secondary aluminium, petroleum refineries, petrochemicals, and textiles) and establishing specific plant-level baselines and targets [2]. This structural expansion significantly increases the demand-side participant pool and the overall complexity and scale of the market, beyond initial expectations of Q4 2025.

The Inevitable Conclusion

IF the Indian Carbon Market's reliance on a nascent pool of Accredited Carbon Verification Agencies (ACVAs) for auditing ~800 obligated industrial plants AND the fact that core registry and exchange infrastructure development is still underway are considered, THEN the market will face significant supply-side constraints for verified Carbon Credit Certificates (CCCs) during its initial active trading period (Q4 2026 – Q1 2027), leading to higher-than-anticipated compliance credit prices due to artificial scarcity and potentially forcing some obligated entities to incur penalties in the first compliance cycle UNLESS the government significantly accelerates ACVA accreditation and provides robust, tested digital infrastructure ahead of schedule and without incident.

Watchlist
01

Publicly released count of Accredited Carbon Verification Agencies (ACVAs) by the Bureau of Energy Efficiency (BEE)

The number of accredited ACVAs directly determines the capacity for verifying GHG emissions from ~800 obligated industrial plants between April and June 2026 [1][4]. A shortage of ACVAs will create a critical bottleneck, delaying the issuance of Carbon Credit Certificates (CCCs) and directly impacting the supply of credits available for trading by October 2026 [1][8].

Watch for: Watch for any official public announcements or lists from BEE detailing the total number, regional distribution, and specific sector accreditations of ACVAs by Q3 2025. A count significantly below 50-100 would indicate a high probability of verification delays and subsequent CCC scarcity.

02

Grid Controller of India (Grid-India) announcements regarding the operational status and pilot testing of the CCC Registry infrastructure

Grid-India is mandated to develop and establish the necessary framework for the CCC Registry, which is the foundational infrastructure for issuance and transfer of credits [5]. Any delays or operational issues in the registry's development or testing will directly impede the ability to issue and trade CCCs, preventing the market from going live as scheduled in October 2026 [1][8].

Watch for: Monitor official press releases, public statements, or any reported progress from Grid-India concerning the registry's development milestones, successful pilot transactions, or integration with power exchanges (IEX/PXIL) by Q1 2026. Lack of clear progress or reported technical difficulties would be a strong negative signal.

Q1/Q2 2026 earnings call commentary and capital expenditure guidance from major Indian industrial companies (e.g., cement, aluminium, refineries, petrochemicals, textiles)

Approximately 800 industrial plants across nine sectors are obligated to design and implement 1-3% reduction plans and overhaul their MRV systems for CCTS compliance starting April 2025 [1][2][6]. Company statements on their readiness, capital allocation for compliance, and internal challenges will be a leading indicator of the overall market's operational friction and potential demand for (or supply of) CCCs. Watch for: Look for specific mentions of CCTS-related capital expenditures, MRV system upgrades, internal training programs, or, conversely, expressions of concern regarding data readiness, regulatory clarity, or verification challenges in their financial disclosures and investor calls for the periods covering 2025-26 compliance year preparation.
